## 1. Introduction

### What is Gunimi?

Gunimi is an AI Business Operating System.

It is not a CRM. It is not a dashboard. It is not a task manager. It is not an AI chat application. It is not a workspace productivity tool.

Gunimi is the operating layer that sits between a business owner and everything their business requires — relationships, deals, meetings, decisions, knowledge, coordination. It understands what is happening. It prepares what comes next. It reduces the time spent managing software so that more time can be spent running the business.

The closest analogy is an exceptional executive assistant who works at the speed of software — one who never forgets, never misses context, always prepares, and never interrupts without a reason.

### What problem does it solve?

Running a business generates overwhelming amounts of context. Customer relationships that span years of emails, meetings, and negotiations. Deals with dozens of moving parts. Team members working in parallel who don't know what each other are doing. Decisions that require information scattered across five different tools. Institutional knowledge that disappears when people leave.

Most business software solves this by giving people better places to store information. A better CRM. A better task manager. A better notes app.

Gunimi solves it differently: by understanding the information that already exists and using it to prepare better decisions, prevent coordination failures, and surface what matters — before being asked.

The goal is not better data storage. The goal is a quieter, more coordinated, more intelligent business.

### Why does it exist?

Most business software was designed in an era when the best a computer could do was store and display information. That era is over. The technology now exists to understand context, notice patterns, prepare recommendations, and coordinate work. Every business deserves access to that capability — not as a separate AI feature, but embedded quietly into every workflow.

Gunimi exists to build that product.

## 2. Vision

### The 2030 Vision

By 2030, Gunimi is the operating system that businesses run inside.

Not a tool they use. Not a platform they log into. An operating system they live inside — the way a company lives inside its accounting system or its email. The difference: this system understands what the company is doing, coordinates the people inside it, and quietly prepares the next best decision.

A founder using Gunimi in 2030 opens their morning and finds everything relevant already prepared: the meeting brief for the 10 AM call, the follow-up email from yesterday's negotiation ready for review, the deal that needs attention before the week ends, and one clear suggested action. They don't search for context. The context was assembled for them.

A sales team using Gunimi knows, without asking, who is already in contact with which client. There are no duplicate emails sent to the same prospect by two different colleagues. There are no deals that stall because the handoff from sales to customer success was unclear. Every workspace carries its own context, its own owner, its own memory.

An enterprise deploying Gunimi has institutional knowledge that survives employee turnover. The playbooks, the relationship history, the negotiation preferences, the customer personalities — these live in the system, not in individual email inboxes or the memory of whoever last worked the account.

### What success looks like

Gunimi has succeeded when a business owner says: "I don't think of it as software anymore."

Gunimi has succeeded when a new team member can be fully effective in their first week because the system already contains the context of every customer, every deal, every decision.

Gunimi has succeeded when the most valuable thing about the system is not the features — it is the accumulated knowledge, the institutional memory, the coordination infrastructure — things that take years to build and make switching away unthinkable.

Gunimi has succeeded when it defines a new software category: not by being better than existing CRMs, but by being a categorically different kind of product.

## 3. Mission

Gunimi's mission is to help businesses spend less time managing software and more time running their business.

### The promises Gunimi makes

**To the business owner:** You will spend your first thirty minutes of every workday knowing exactly what matters. You will not search for context. You will not wonder what happened last time. You will not lose information when someone leaves the company.

**To the sales team:** You will never accidentally contact a client that a colleague is already managing. You will always know who owns a relationship. You will have the context you need before every meeting, without preparing for it yourself.

**To the operations leader:** Accountability will always be clear. Every workspace will have one visible owner. Handoffs will be structured and documented. Coordination failures will be caught before they become customer problems.

**To the enterprise:** Your institutional knowledge will outlive your employees. Your best practices will be encoded, shared, and applied automatically. Your AI will respect your permissions, never expose information to the wrong people, and always explain why it recommends something.

**To every user:** This software will never shout at you. It will not send you notifications that don't require your attention. It will not manufacture urgency. It will be quiet, confident, and useful — the way the best professional tools always are.

## 4. Core Values

### Trust

Gunimi earns trust by never lying, never exaggerating, and never inventing urgency that doesn't exist. When data is insufficient, Gunimi says so. When nothing requires attention, Gunimi says nothing. Every recommendation comes with an explanation grounded in evidence. Every AI output can be reviewed, corrected, and overridden. Trust is earned slowly and lost instantly — Gunimi treats it accordingly.

### Clarity

Business clarity is the product. Every screen, every feature, every AI output exists to make the user's business situation more legible. If something adds confusion — adds a new concept, adds a new navigation item, adds a notification — it fails this value. Before adding, always ask: does this make the situation clearer or more complicated?

### Calmness

Gunimi never shouts. It does not use alert colors for routine events. It does not surface information that requires no action. It does not animate things that do not need to move. The product has confidence — and confidence is quiet. Users should feel less anxious after using Gunimi, not more.

### Business-First

Every design decision starts with a business problem, not a software pattern. Users think about customers, deals, opportunities, and decisions. They never think about modules, navigation structures, or where to click to find information. The software organizes itself around how business owners think, not around how the database is structured.

### Context

The most valuable thing Gunimi can provide is the right context at the right moment. Not all the information — the right information. A salesperson opening a deal workspace needs to know what matters about this deal right now, not a complete history of every field edit. Context requires curation, and curation requires intelligence.

### Coordination

The most expensive failures in business are not productivity failures. They are coordination failures: the duplicate email sent to the same client, the deal that stalled because no one was watching it, the customer who was promised something that the CS team didn't know about. Gunimi's highest-value capability is reducing coordination cost — not making individuals faster, but making teams more coherent.

### Collaboration

Every feature is designed for teams. Solo mode is collaboration with one participant. Nothing about Gunimi's architecture should need to change as a company grows from one person to one thousand. The mental model is stable. The capabilities grow.

### Human Judgment

Gunimi prepares decisions. It never makes them. Every recommendation is a suggestion, not an instruction. Every automated action requires approval. The human is always in control. Gunimi exists to make that control more informed, not to replace it.

### Transparency

Gunimi never acts secretly. When AI generates something, the user knows it was generated. When an action is taken automatically, the user can see it and reverse it. When a recommendation is made, the reasoning is visible. The system has no hidden behaviors.

## 5. Product Philosophy

### The Invisible Assistant

The best AI interaction is the one where the user never thinks about using AI.

They open a meeting workspace and the brief is already there. They look at a deal and the health assessment is already written. They arrive at their desk in the morning and the day's priorities are already prepared. At no point did they click "Generate" or "Analyze." It was simply done.

This is the Invisible Assistant principle. AI is not a button. AI is not a chatbot. AI is not a destination. AI is an infrastructure layer that operates beneath every workflow, preparing things before they are needed and staying invisible when they are not.

The test: would a first-time user know that AI generated this? If the experience is natural and the information is accurate, they might not. That is success.

### Prepare Decisions

Gunimi prepares judgment. It does not replace it.

This distinction matters. A system that replaces judgment makes users dependent and reduces accountability. A system that prepares judgment makes users more capable and increases their effectiveness.

Gunimi may summarize, draft, recommend, explain, organize, and coordinate. But important business decisions — who to hire, whether to close a deal, how to respond to a difficult customer — always belong to the humans inside the business.

### Business Before Software

Users think about customers, deals, meetings, and opportunities. They never think about modules or where data is stored.

Every time the product requires a user to think about the software rather than their business, that is a failure. Navigation should reflect business intent. Pages should reflect business context. Recommendations should reflect business evidence.

If a feature is named after its database table (Contacts, Notes, Activities), that is a warning sign. If a feature is named after what users are trying to do (People, Conversations, Today's Work), that is the right direction.

### Context Before Modules

Nobody wakes up thinking "I should open CRM." They think "I need to close Acme."

Every feature in Gunimi is designed around business context. The question is never "which module does this data belong to?" The question is "what is the user trying to accomplish right now, and what context do they need to accomplish it?"

A deal, a customer relationship, a meeting — these are complete contexts. Everything needed to work on them should be present in one place.

### Calm Software

Confidence is quiet.

The most trustworthy systems are also the calmest. They do not alert users unnecessarily. They do not animate things that don't need to move. They do not manufacture urgency to appear more useful. They simply work — and when they work well, they fade into the background.

Gunimi is ambitious software with quiet confidence. It never shouts. The absence of noise is part of the product.

### Executive Assistant Test

Every feature must answer three questions before it is built:

1. Would this genuinely help a business owner?
2. Would an exceptional executive assistant behave like this?
3. Would this still feel natural with 500 concurrent users?

If any answer is No, redesign the feature before building it.

The executive assistant analogy is precise. An exceptional assistant notices what you need, prepares it before you ask, explains their reasoning briefly, and never interrupts you unless the interruption is worth it. They have a strong sense of what matters and what doesn't. They do not bring you every piece of information — they bring you the right piece at the right moment.

That is how Gunimi should behave.

### North Star

Every product decision should answer one question:

*Does this help people think less about software and more about their business?*

If yes, continue. If not, rethink.

## 7. AI Philosophy

### Role of AI

AI in Gunimi is infrastructure, not a feature.

The distinction matters. A feature is something users interact with by choice — they click on it, they open it, they invoke it. Infrastructure is something they rely on without thinking about it. Roads are infrastructure. Electricity is infrastructure. The spell-checker in a word processor is infrastructure.

Gunimi's AI is infrastructure. It operates beneath every workflow. It prepares things before they are needed. It coordinates people without being asked to. It surfaces information that is relevant without being told what is relevant. Users benefit from it continuously without consciously using it.

This means: no "AI" navigation item. No "Generate Insights" button for routine intelligence. No chatbot that the user must open to get value. The AI appears only when it creates value — and when it does, it does so quietly.

### Behavior

Gunimi's AI behaves like an exceptional executive assistant:

**It notices.** Before being asked, it observes patterns, detects staleness, identifies conflicts, and recognizes when something requires attention. It doesn't wait for a trigger — it pays attention continuously.

**It prepares.** It assembles context before the user needs it. The meeting brief is ready 2 hours before the meeting. The deal health assessment is pre-loaded when the workspace opens. The follow-up email is drafted while the meeting notes are being written.

**It explains.** Every recommendation comes with a reason grounded in evidence. Not "this deal needs attention" but "this deal needs attention because there has been no activity in 12 days and the proposal is still unanswered." The explanation is always in plain language.

**It recommends.** Suggestions are specific, actionable, and grounded in context. Not "consider following up" but "I'd send Maria the updated contract today — she mentioned the board meeting is on Friday."

**It coordinates.** Before any outreach action, it checks whether a teammate is already working on the same contact. Before any task is created, it checks whether a similar task already exists. Before a deal moves to a new stage, it checks whether the required approvals are in place.

**It waits for approval.** AI never sends, publishes, or executes consequential actions without explicit human confirmation. It prepares; humans approve.

**It never interrupts without purpose.** Every AI-triggered notification is Tier 1 — it requires a decision or an action. AI-generated awareness information surfaces in digests, not real-time interruptions.

### Limitations

Gunimi's AI must always operate within clear limits:

- It never invents data. If information is not available, it says "I don't have enough information to assess this."
- It never exaggerates urgency. If nothing requires attention, the screen is quiet.
- It never exposes information to users who don't have access to it. Permission boundaries are absolute — AI does not reason across permission walls.
- It never makes consequential decisions without human approval. Sending an email, updating a stage, assigning ownership — these always require explicit confirmation.
- It never pretends to understand more than it does. Confidence is expressed proportionally to the evidence available.

### Memory

Memory is not chat history.

When Gunimi remembers something about a customer, it doesn't mean the AI has a log of past conversations. It means the system holds structured business knowledge: this customer prefers direct communication, this deal was lost because the budget was reallocated, this company has a procurement process that requires 60-day lead time.

Business memory has three layers:
- **Personal memory** — individual user preferences and communication style (private)
- **Workspace memory** — synthesized intelligence about a specific entity, shared with everyone who has workspace access
- **Institutional memory** — decisions, playbooks, patterns, and knowledge that survive individual employees

Memory must be accurate. Memory must be auditable. Memory must survive people.

### Tone of Voice

AI outputs in Gunimi use a specific voice:

- **Direct:** "3 deals need attention" — not "We found 3 deals that may need your attention"
- **Specific:** "Revenue down 12% this week" — not "Revenue metric changed"
- **Present tense:** "Analyzing workspace data" — not "Analysis in progress"
- **Action-oriented:** "I'd schedule a follow-up with Acme" — not "Consider a follow-up"
- **Calm:** Never urgent, never alarming, never insistent unless urgency is warranted
- **No filler:** Never "Please wait while we…", "We're working on…", "Almost done…"
- **Never announce the AI:** No "As your AI assistant…", no "AI analysis shows…", no "Powered by AI"

The voice is a trusted colleague who has already done the thinking — not a chatbot who is performing helpfulness.

### Reasoning

Gunimi's AI always reasons from the specific to the general — never the reverse.

A recommendation for a specific deal comes from specific evidence about that deal. A health signal for a specific customer comes from observable facts about that relationship. The AI does not apply generic heuristics and call them personalized insights.

Every AI output must be traceable: if the user asks "why did you recommend this?", the answer exists in the data and can be shown.

## 8. Collaboration Philosophy

### The Scale Principle

Every feature in Gunimi is designed to work at 1 user, 5 users, 20 users, 100 users, and 1000 users — without redesigning the mental model.

This is not aspirational. It is a constraint on every design decision. Before a feature ships, it must be evaluated: does this still make sense at 100 users? At 1000? Does it create noise that scales with team size? Does it require manual curation that becomes unmanageable?

Features that work at small scale but break at large scale are not "good enough for now." They are architectural debt that will need to be paid.

### Presence

Presence is workspace-scoped.

When a team member is actively working in the same workspace — the same Deal, the same Customer, the same Company — their presence is indicated. This is the minimal signal needed to prevent the most common coordination failure: two people working on the same thing simultaneously without knowing.

Presence is not "who is online." Presence is not a global activity feed. Presence is workspace-contextual awareness and nothing more.

### Ownership

Every workspace has exactly one owner at all times.

Ownership is not a metadata field. It is the accountability backbone of the entire system. When ownership is unclear, coordination failures multiply: nobody knows who makes the final call, nobody knows who to notify about a change, nobody knows who is responsible when something falls through the cracks.

Ownership must be:
- Visible — always shown in the workspace header, never buried
- Transferable — with a structured workflow that requires both parties to confirm
- Historical — every ownership change is logged with a timestamp and a context note
- Escalatable — defined escalation paths when an owner is unreachable

### Intent Broadcasting

Before taking an action, signal that you intend to take it.

When a user begins composing an email to a contact, that intent is broadcast to other members who have access to that workspace. If a teammate opened a compose window for the same contact in the last thirty minutes, a soft warning appears.

Intent broadcasting is not a lock. It is not a blocker. It is a soft awareness signal that prevents coordination failures without creating bureaucratic friction.

### Conflict Prevention

The AI detects situations where duplicate work is likely before it happens.

When two team members are composing emails to the same contact in the same window: warning.
When a task is created that closely resembles an existing task for the same entity: suggestion to merge.
When a deal moves to a stage that requires a handoff: automatic prompt to initiate the handoff workflow.
When an approval-gated action is triggered: the action is queued, not blocked, and the approver is notified.

Conflicts surface before they become problems. Not after.

### Role Awareness

The same workspace, different perspectives.

A CEO opening a deal workspace sees: decisions required, strategic risk, exceptions.
A salesperson opening the same workspace sees: next actions, email history, relationship health.
A finance lead opening the same workspace sees: deal value, discount history, revenue impact.
A customer success manager opening the same workspace sees: relationship signals, renewal risk, onboarding status.

None of these users see different data. They see the same data — organized, prioritized, and surfaced according to what matters for their role. Role is not access control. Role is context curation.

### Shared Memory

Institutional knowledge must survive people.

When a sales team member leaves, their customer context should not leave with them. Their email patterns, their relationship notes, their customer preferences, their deal history — all of this should transfer to the workspace memory, available to whoever takes ownership next.

Business memory is not stored in inboxes. It is not stored in Slack threads. It lives in the workspace, structured, searchable, and owned by the organization.

## 9. Workspace Philosophy

### Context-First Workspaces

The workspace is the fundamental unit of Gunimi.

Not the module. Not the database table. Not the feature. The workspace.

A workspace is a complete working environment organized around a business context — a customer, a deal, a company, a meeting. It contains everything needed to complete work on that context: the AI brief, the conversation history, the related business objects, the tasks, the notes, the approvals, and the memory of everything that has happened.

The test for a workspace: can a user open it and complete their entire workflow without navigating away? If yes, it is a workspace. If no, it is a detail page — and it needs to evolve.

### Workspace Structure

Every workspace has the same architecture:

- **Header:** Entity name, type, owner badge, health signal, primary actions
- **Tabs:** Exactly 4, contextual to the entity type
- **AI context panel:** Pre-loaded, right-side, collapsible — a brief about this entity, prepared before the user arrived
- **Presence indicators:** Who else is in this workspace right now

Tab count is not flexible. Exactly 4, always. Consistency across workspace types is what makes the product feel like an operating system — users learn one mental model and it applies everywhere.

### Workspace Lifecycle

Workspaces are not created and forgotten. They evolve.

A new customer relationship begins as a prospect contact workspace — minimal context, a few touchpoints. Over time, it accumulates meeting notes, email history, deal associations, and AI-synthesized intelligence. A three-year customer workspace is materially richer than a three-week one, and the system reflects that.

Workspace health signals reflect this lifecycle. A workspace in early stages is assessed honestly: "Limited history available." A workspace with rich history is assessed richly: "This relationship has been consistently warm for 18 months, with an active expansion opportunity."

### Workspace Ownership

Ownership is always visible. Ownership always belongs to one person.

The workspace header displays the owner. The ownership history is accessible. Transfers are structured — both parties confirm. The AI monitors for ownership staleness and alerts the appropriate manager when an owner is inactive.

No workspace is ever ownerless. If an owner leaves the company, their workspaces are automatically escalated to their manager and flagged for reassignment.

### Workspace Memory

Every workspace has its own intelligence layer.

When a user opens a Customer Workspace, the AI context panel is already populated with: a natural language summary of the relationship, the last 3–5 significant events, the current health signal, and one suggested next action. This is not generated on demand — it is prepared when the workspace data changes.

Workspace memory is shared by all users with workspace access. It is not personal memory. When the account owner changes, the new owner inherits the full context.

### Workspace Evolution

Workspaces grow in capability as the product evolves.

In the early product, a workspace may have limited AI context. In later phases, it will have full meeting intelligence, collaboration features, approval workflows, and institutional memory synthesis. The shell — the header, the tabs, the structure — remains constant. The intelligence inside it deepens over time.

## 10. Design Philosophy

### The Gunimi Design Language (GDL)

Gunimi has one design language. Not two. Not a page-specific variant. One.

The GDL defines every visual decision: colors, typography, spacing, radius, shadows, motion, surfaces, and components. Nothing is hardcoded in a component. Every value references a design token. If a token does not exist, it must be added to the design language before being used.

The GDL is not updated casually. It is extended deliberately.

### Visual Identity

Gunimi is dark-first, premium, and enterprise-minimal.

**Dark-first** means the default experience is optimized for dark backgrounds. The palette is built around deep space (`#05060A`) as the page floor, titanium (`#0A0E17`) as the standard surface, and progressively lighter surfaces for elevation.

**Premium** means restraint. Not minimalism for its own sake — restraint with purpose. Every element earns its place. Decoration that does not reduce cognitive load does not belong.

**Enterprise-minimal** means the product looks equally credible to a solo founder and the CTO of a Fortune 500 company. No startup gradients. No consumer-app friendly colors. No rounded-corners-everywhere aesthetic. Sophisticated, precise, confident.

### Color System

The color system is semantic. Every color has a meaning.

- **Violet** (`--g-primary`, `#6D5BFF`) — primary actions, brand identity, selection states
- **Cyan** (`--g-ai`, `#22D3EE`) — AI-only elements, exclusively. Never used for non-AI UI.
- **Text scale** (`--g-text` through `--g-muted`) — hierarchy, not decoration
- **Status colors** — success, warning, danger, info — always paired with text labels, never used alone

Cyan is a semantic signal: "AI is operating here." If an element uses cyan and the AI is not involved, the color is wrong.

All shadows are ambient purple (`rgba(109,91,255,...)`). Black shadows do not exist in Gunimi. The interface is illuminated from within.

### Typography

One font: Inter.

Headings use tight negative tracking (`-0.02em` to `-0.06em` depending on size). Display text uses tight line-height (`1.0–1.1`). Body text is 14px, readable, and unhurried. Numbers are always monospaced (`font-mono`).

Nothing below 13px for interactive or readable content. Nothing below 11px anywhere. Every typographic decision reduces cognitive load — denser when context demands it, spacious when clarity requires it.

### Spacing

4px grid. No exceptions.

Every spacing value in the product is a multiple of 4: 4px, 8px, 12px, 16px, 20px, 24px, 32px, 40px, 48px, 64px, 80px, 96px, 128px. Nothing outside this scale. No `px-5 py-3` — those values conflict (20px and 12px are on grid, but combined as irregular padding they create inconsistency). Use `px-4 py-3` or `px-5 py-4`.

### Motion

Motion communicates. It does not decorate.

Every transition should feel inevitable, not clever. Users should not notice animations — they should only notice that the interface feels smooth. If someone watches an animation and thinks "nice animation," it may be too prominent.

Speed range: 80ms (instant state changes) to 650ms (page entrances). Nothing faster than 80ms; nothing slower than 650ms for UI motion. AI elements operate on a separate timescale: 3.2s–88s for orbital rings and breathing effects.

Hover states should feel like they have weight. The slowest acceptable transition is preferred over the fastest.

All animations respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. This is not optional.

### Surface Hierarchy

Four surface levels. Never skip them.

- L0: `#05060A` — page floor, sidebar
- L1: `#0A0E17` — standard card, content surface
- L2: `#0F1520` — elevated card
- L3: `#161E2E` — float, nested surface

An L3 element placed directly on an L0 background looks visually disconnected. Surface hierarchy is spatial depth — it communicates structure and containment.

### Accessibility

Gunimi is WCAG AA compliant across its primary text combinations and AAA where feasible.

Every interactive element has a visible focus ring (keyboard navigation is a first-class concern, not an afterthought). Every status indicated by color is also indicated by text. Every icon-only button has an `aria-label`. Live AI states use `aria-live="polite"`. Modal dialogs trap focus and support Escape to close.

Accessibility is not a compliance checkbox. It is part of what makes the product premium. Software that cannot be used by people who rely on assistive technology is not premium software.

### Consistency Over Creativity

When implementing a new UI surface, the first question is: does the GDL already have a component for this? If yes, use it.

Page-specific design patterns are prohibited. If a pattern is needed that does not exist in the GDL, add it to the GDL first — not to the page.

The reason: every one-off design pattern increases the cognitive load of future developers who need to maintain it. Every component built on the shared system reduces that load. Consistency is not a creative limitation; it is a quality multiplier.

## 11. Engineering Philosophy

### Production-Ready Code Only

There is no "V1 implementation." There is no "temporary solution." There is no "we'll clean this up later."

Every line of code shipped to users is production code. It should be written with the assumption that it will be read by a future engineer who does not know why it was written. It should be maintainable, testable, and correct.

This is not about perfectionism. It is about the compounding cost of technical debt. One temporary solution creates one place where future work is harder. Ten temporary solutions create a codebase that is actively hostile to change.

### Localization — The First Rule

**No hardcoded user-facing strings. Ever.**

Every visible label, button, heading, description, error message, success message, empty state, tooltip, and placeholder must live in the locale files (`locales/en.json`, `locales/sk.json`, `locales/cs.json`).

This is Rule #1 because it cannot be retrofitted. A product built with hardcoded strings requires a full rewrite to internationalize. A product built with localization from the start can ship in any language with no structural changes.

Three locales are always required: English, Slovak, Czech. Every key added to one must be added to all three with authentic translations. Not machine translations used as placeholders — authentic translations.

**Server components** use `const t = await getTranslations('namespace')` from `next-intl/server`.
**Client components** use `const t = useTranslations('namespace')` from `next-intl`.
**Data arrays with text** use `t.raw('key') as SomeType[]` — never inline arrays.

Brand terms that are never translated: Gunimi, AI Core, Workspace, Command Center, Memory, Observatory, Automation.

### Production Logger Policy — The Second Rule

**Production never outputs developer logging to the browser console.**

`console.log`, `console.debug`, and `console.info` are forbidden in all files. `console.error` is forbidden in client components. Server-side infrastructure failures use `logger.error()` from `lib/logger.ts`.

Caught errors in client code use `catch { }` with no variable unless the error object is actually used. Sentry captures all unhandled errors automatically.

The reason: browser console output in production exposes implementation details to every user who opens DevTools, creates security risk, and signals unprofessionalism. Premium software does not log to the console.

### Quality Gates — The Third Rule

A feature is not complete until all of the following pass:

| Gate | Standard |
|------|----------|
| TypeScript | `npm run type-check` — zero errors |
| ESLint | `npm run lint` — zero errors, no new warnings |
| Production Build | `npm run build` — compiles clean |
| Localization | Every visible string in all three locale files |
| GDL Compliance | Uses GDL components, no page-specific design patterns |
| Runtime | Browser console clean, no unhandled errors, no 404s on expected assets |

These gates are not suggestions. They are the definition of "done." Code that does not pass all gates is not shipped. Code that introduces new ESLint warnings is treated the same as code that fails TypeScript — it does not pass.

### Architecture Principles

**Server Actions are the data layer.** Database access goes through server actions. Client components receive typed data as props. Business logic lives in server actions, not in components.

**Supabase RLS is enforced.** User-facing queries use `createClient()`. Admin queries use `supabaseAdmin`. RLS policies are the security boundary — they are never bypassed for convenience.

**Do not change the database schema unless explicitly requested.** Schema changes have broad impact. They require migration, RLS review, and type regeneration. No schema change should be made as a side effect of a feature implementation.

**Do not duplicate logic.** Before implementing something, check whether it already exists. The codebase has server actions, utilities, and hooks. Use them. Duplicate logic creates two places to maintain and two places to introduce bugs.

**OrbitOS components are the UI standard.** Use existing components: OrbitPage, OrbitSection, OrbitHeading, OrbitCard, OrbitMetricCard, OrbitTimeline, OrbitDialog, OrbitTable, OrbitEmptyState. Do not invent page-specific equivalents.

### Performance

Performance is a product quality, not a technical afterthought.

Every server component is as fast as the slowest data fetch on its critical path. Parallel data fetching is the default — sequential fetches that could be parallel are a performance bug.

Client-side state is minimal. Server components render the majority of the page. Client components are islands of interactivity inside server-rendered shells.

AI responses that take more than 1 second should show a meaningful loading state — not a spinner, but a contextual phrase that communicates what is being prepared.

### Security

Security decisions are never shortcuts.

SQL injection is impossible: Supabase client parameters are always parameterized. XSS is impossible: React's JSX escaping handles user-generated content, and no `dangerouslySetInnerHTML` is used unless explicitly reviewed. CSRF is handled by the Next.js server action architecture. RLS policies are the primary access control boundary.

No secrets in client code. No API keys in environment variables accessible to the browser. Security is reviewed at the boundary — input validation at system edges, trust inside the system.

### Maintainability

Code is written for the next person who reads it.

Comments explain why, never what. Well-named identifiers explain what — comments do not need to repeat them. A comment that says `// Get the user` above `const user = getUser()` is noise. A comment that says `// Supabase returns null for missing rows — use maybeSingle() here` is signal.

No premature abstractions. Three similar lines are better than a wrapper function that adds complexity for hypothetical future use cases. Abstractions earn their existence by being used in at least three places with meaningfully different contexts.

## 12. User Experience Principles

### Reduce Cognitive Load

Every interaction with Gunimi should leave the user feeling more informed and less overwhelmed — never the reverse.

Information is curated, not dumped. The AI surfaces what matters, not everything it knows. Screens show what is relevant to today's work, not every historical record. Navigation reflects what users are trying to do, not the structure of the underlying system.

When a screen feels busy, the first response is subtraction, not reorganization.

### One Recommendation Beats Ten Suggestions

A system that surfaces one clear recommendation earns more trust than a system that surfaces ten suggestions and leaves the choice to the user.

The executive assistant test applies: would an exceptional assistant give you a ranked list of ten options, or would they say "I'd do this — here's why"? The answer is the latter. Gunimi surfaces one priority, one suggestion, one next action — and explains the reasoning. This requires confidence. The product should have it.

### Never Interrupt

Every notification must pass a threshold: does this require the user's attention right now?

If the answer is no — if it is awareness information, historical context, or general system activity — it does not interrupt. It surfaces in a digest, in a workspace activity feed, or not at all.

Interruptions that do not require immediate action train users to ignore all notifications. Once notification trust is lost, it cannot be recovered.

### Never Overwhelm

The product never shows everything it knows.

A customer with five years of history does not need to show five years of history. It needs to show what is relevant right now, with access to history when the user wants it.

Scroll-through pages that stack all sections regardless of content are an anti-pattern. Workspaces with tabs that surface relevant content per context are the standard.

### Empty States Are Acceptable

When there is nothing to show, show nothing.

An empty state should not manufacture content, generate placeholder suggestions, or create artificial busyness to fill the screen. If a user has no tasks due today, the Tasks section of Today is simply not shown. Silence is honest.

This also applies to AI. When the AI does not have sufficient context to make a meaningful assessment, it says so. "Limited information available — add more contact history to improve this assessment." This is more valuable than a fabricated confidence signal.

### Business Language Always

User-facing text uses business language, not software language.

"People" not "Contacts." "Pipeline" not "Deals." "Today" not "Dashboard." "Workspace" not "Settings." The words users see reflect how business owners think — not how the database is modeled.

## 13. Information Architecture

### Current Direction

Gunimi's navigation is evolving from a module-based structure (organized by database table) to a context-first structure (organized by business intent).

The destination is a six-item navigation:

- **Today** — The morning briefing and the day's work. What matters right now.
- **Pipeline** — All deals and opportunities, viewed as business progress, not records.
- **People** — Contacts and companies, unified as relationship context.
- **Inbox** — New and unassigned communications.
- **Work** — Tasks anchored to their business contexts.
- **Workspace** — Configuration, members, preferences, and administrative management.

Everything else is absorbed into workspaces, accessible through context, not navigation.

### Why Context-First Exists

Module-based navigation creates a fundamental user experience problem: users must hold two mental models simultaneously — what they are trying to accomplish, and which module contains the information they need.

"I need to follow up on the Acme deal. Is the email thread in Email? Is the deal context in Deals? Are my notes in Notes? Are my tasks in Tasks?"

In a context-first architecture, the Acme Deal Workspace contains all of this. One navigation item. One context. Complete work without leaving.

### How Navigation Evolves

Navigation items are removed when their content moves into workspaces.

Notes: absorbed into entity workspaces (every Customer, Company, and Deal Workspace has a notes capability).
Activity: absorbed into workspace activity feeds.
Memory: absorbed into workspace AI context panels.
Automations: absorbed into Workspace configuration.
AI: removed entirely — it is never a destination.

The sidebar shrinks as the product matures. A shorter sidebar is a sign of progress, not incompleteness.

### Workspace Navigation

Within workspaces, navigation is always four tabs. No more. No less.

The four tabs differ by workspace type but follow a consistent pattern:
- Overview (health, AI brief, quick context)
- Conversations or Business (the domain-specific primary view)
- Activity or People (relationships and history)
- Work (tasks, follow-ups, action items)

The consistency of this structure across all workspace types is what makes the product feel like an operating system. Users learn the pattern once.

## 14. Roadmap Philosophy

### Why Phases Exist

The product is built in phases not because of resource constraints, but because of architectural dependencies. Some features cannot be built until the foundation they depend on exists.

Context panels cannot be built until workspaces exist. Coordination AI cannot work until presence and ownership are established. Institutional memory cannot function until workspace memory has been in use long enough to be meaningful.

Each phase completes a layer. The next phase builds on top of that layer.

### Phase 0 — Immediate Quick Wins

The purpose of Phase 0 is to remove things that actively contradict the OS vision.

An AI navigation item that declares "AI is a destination." A Memory page that makes AI visible when it should be invisible. A global activity feed that creates noise where there should be signal. Analytics widgets on a "Today" screen that turns it into a metrics overview.

Phase 0 requires no new development. It requires courage — the willingness to remove things that took time to build because they undermine the product's identity.

This phase exists first because first impressions are set by what users see when they log in. If the navigation contradicts the product's philosophy, no amount of AI capability fixes the first impression.

### Phase 1 — Open Alpha Foundations

The purpose of Phase 1 is to make the product coherent enough for public sharing.

The Today screen becomes genuinely "today." The navigation becomes business-oriented. The first behavioral step toward workspaces (slide-over context navigation) establishes the pattern that Phase 2 will complete. Ownership is visible on every entity.

A new user should be able to understand what the product does by reading the navigation alone. That is the Phase 1 milestone.

### Phase 2 — Workspace Evolution

The purpose of Phase 2 is to transform detail pages into workspaces.

This is the most significant architectural change in the product's history. It is where Gunimi stops being "a CRM with an AI panel" and starts being "an operating system where every business entity is a complete working environment."

The phase is defined by one test: can a salesperson manage an entire deal — from first contact to close — without leaving the Deal Workspace? When the answer is yes, Phase 2 is complete.

### Phase 3 — Invisible Assistant

The purpose of Phase 3 is to make AI present everywhere and visible nowhere.

The AI context panel in every workspace, pre-loaded before the user asks. The meeting brief, ready before the meeting starts. The post-meeting follow-up, drafted while the notes are being written. The floating chatbot button replaced by keyboard-driven intelligence.

After Phase 3, users should occasionally feel a small surprise: "How did it already know this?" That surprise is the desired outcome. It is the invisible assistant principle made tangible.

### Phase 4 — Collaboration Layer

The purpose of Phase 4 is to make Gunimi safe and effective for teams.

The most expensive collaboration failures are not visible until they happen: two salespeople emailing the same prospect, a deal stalling because the handoff was never completed, a customer problem falling through the cracks because ownership was ambiguous.

Phase 4 prevents these failures before they happen — through ownership enforcement, presence awareness, intent broadcasting, and coordination AI.

After Phase 4, a team of twenty can use Gunimi for thirty days without a duplicate outreach incident. That is the milestone.

### Phase 5 — Operating System

The purpose of Phase 5 is to complete the product's identity as a category.

The Command Center. Shared institutional memory. Predictive surfacing. Workspace templates. The Living Today.

After Phase 5, Gunimi is not competing with CRMs. It is in a different category: an AI Business Operating System where switching away means losing years of institutional knowledge, coordination infrastructure, and AI context. The switching cost is not contractual — it is knowledge-based.

### Future Vision

The product evolves. The principles do not.

Future capabilities — a spatial interface, multi-agent coordination, voice interaction, industry-specific workspace templates, a public API for third-party workspace builders — should be evaluated against every principle in this document before they are built.

If a future capability violates a principle, either the capability is redesigned or it is not built. The principles are not negotiated for features.

## 15. Decision Framework

When a feature is proposed, evaluate it against six questions before implementation begins.

### 1. Does it reduce complexity?

Does the feature simplify the product — fewer screens to navigate, fewer concepts to learn, less information to process? Or does it add a new concept, a new navigation item, a new cognitive burden?

If the answer is "it adds complexity for a specific group of users while being irrelevant to others," evaluate whether the complexity can be contained (shown only to relevant users, accessible only in relevant workspaces) or whether it should not be built.

### 2. Does it reduce context switching?

Does the feature allow users to complete more of their work without navigating away? Or does it send them somewhere else to get the information they need?

Context switching has a measurable cognitive cost. Every navigation change breaks flow. A feature that adds useful information inside an existing workspace is better than a feature that adds a new page with the same information.

### 3. Does it improve collaboration?

Does the feature work better with five users than with one? With twenty than with five?

If the feature does not scale — if it creates noise at scale, requires manual coordination at scale, or breaks the mental model at scale — it should be redesigned before being built.

### 4. Would an executive assistant do this?

Would an exceptional executive assistant, with access to all the information Gunimi has, make this available to the user in the way this feature proposes?

If the feature involves surfacing information unprompted, is this the information an executive assistant would surface? Is this the moment they would surface it? Is this how they would phrase it?

If the feature involves taking an action on behalf of the user, would an executive assistant take this action without being asked? Or would they prepare it and wait for approval?

### 5. Would it scale to 500 concurrent users?

If 500 people are actively using this feature simultaneously, does the experience degrade? Does the feature create noise proportional to user count? Does the data model hold?

If the feature works well at one user but creates an unusable experience at five hundred, it is not a feature — it is a prototype.

### 6. Would it still make sense in 2030?

Is this feature tied to a current technology trend, or does it address a permanent human need?

AI features that depend on a specific model version, specific API behavior, or specific technology capability are fragile. Features that address permanent needs — preparing decisions, reducing coordination cost, surfacing relevant context — are durable.

If the answer to this question requires predicting specific technology developments, the feature is probably too implementation-specific. Abstract it to the principle it serves.

**If any answer is No: redesign before building.**

## 16. The Gunimi Pledge

This pledge is permanent. It describes how Gunimi will be built, regardless of the technology, the team, the market pressure, or the competitive landscape.

---

We will never build AI for marketing.

AI in Gunimi creates genuine value in workflows — it does not create the appearance of AI to differentiate in a category. If an AI feature does not help a business owner make a better decision or avoid a costly mistake, it does not ship.

---

We will never interrupt without value.

Every notification, every proactive AI surface, every automatic action is held to the standard: does this require the user's attention right now? If the answer is no, it does not interrupt. Notification trust, once lost, is not recoverable.

---

We will never replace human judgment.

Every consequential decision belongs to the human. Gunimi prepares, drafts, recommends, and organizes. It never decides on behalf of the business. The human is always in control — and Gunimi's goal is to make that control more informed, not to remove it.

---

We will never expose unnecessary complexity.

Every concept in the product must earn its place. Every navigation item, every new screen, every new feature adds cognitive load. We subtract before we add. We absorb before we expose. Simplicity is not a design preference — it is a product commitment.

---

We will always explain recommendations.

Every AI suggestion answers the question "Why?" with evidence from business context. Never "AI thinks this is important." Always "This deal needs attention because there has been no activity in 12 days and the proposal is unanswered." The reasoning is always visible, always honest, always grounded.

---

We will always optimize for trust before automation.

Automation earns its role gradually. First Gunimi prepares. Then it recommends. Then, once trust is established, it acts — and only within the bounds the user has defined. Trust is the precondition for every degree of autonomy.

---

We will always design for teams.

Solo mode is collaboration with one participant. Every feature is evaluated for its behavior at scale: 1 user, 5 users, 20 users, 100 users, 1000 users. Features that create coordination problems at scale are redesigned before they ship.

---

We will always protect business context.

Context is the product. The email about the Acme deal belongs inside the Acme deal workspace. The meeting notes from last Tuesday belong connected to the contacts who were in that meeting. Context must not be fragmented across modules. Context must not be lost when people leave. Context must survive.

---

We will always preserve shared knowledge.

Institutional knowledge — the playbooks, the relationship history, the decisions made and why, the patterns that work — belongs to the organization, not to individual employees. When people leave, their knowledge stays. When systems change, the knowledge transfers. Business memory is not a feature; it is a responsibility.

---

We will always remember that businesses are run by people.

Every business on Gunimi is run by people who are trying to accomplish something real: build a company, serve customers, close deals, build a team. The product exists to help those people. Every decision — technical, product, design, AI — is ultimately a decision about whether it helps them do that better.

---

*These principles were written to outlive every framework, every model, every team member, and every trend. If Gunimi continues to follow this philosophy, it will not become another SaaS platform. It will become the operating system businesses rely on every day.*
