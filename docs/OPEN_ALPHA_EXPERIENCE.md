# Gunimi Open Alpha Experience

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Foundational — permanent
**Audience:** Product · Design · Growth · Every future contributor to the first experience

---

> *"The best first impression is not impressive. It is trustworthy."*

---

This document defines the complete first experience of Gunimi — from the first moment a person encounters the product until the moment they complete their first real piece of work inside it.

It is not a marketing plan.

It is not an onboarding checklist.

It is a philosophy for how a product earns the right to exist in someone's working life.

If any future first-experience design conflicts with this document, the design changes. This document does not.

---

## 1. Philosophy

### Why this experience exists

Every person who discovers Gunimi has already tried something else.

They have used CRMs that turned into graveyards of outdated data. They have tried productivity tools that required more maintenance than the work itself. They have opened AI features that promised to change how they work and delivered a chatbot that interrupted them. They have been promised clarity by software that delivered complexity.

They are not naive. They have been disappointed before. They arrive with guarded optimism — willing to be convinced, but no longer willing to be fooled.

This is the person we are designing for.

Our first obligation is not to impress them. It is to not betray them. Every other software product they have tried has betrayed them in the first five minutes — with overwhelming dashboards, mandatory tutorials, fabricated value, or pressure to commit before they have understood anything.

Gunimi's first experience is a promise kept, not a promise made.

### The goal

The goal of the first experience is not conversion. It is not activation. It is not retention. It is not any metric.

The goal is this: after two minutes with Gunimi, the user should feel that something has clicked into place. Not excitement. Not amazement. Something quieter and more durable: the feeling that this is how software was supposed to feel all along.

That feeling is trust. And trust, once earned in the first two minutes, compounds in ways that no feature ever can.

### Desired emotional outcome

After two minutes with Gunimi, the user should feel:

**Calm.** Not overwhelmed. Not pressured. The pace of the experience should match the pace of serious work — deliberate, unhurried, purposeful.

**Understood.** The product should speak about business problems the user recognizes — not abstract features, not technical capabilities. The language should feel like it was written for them specifically.

**Curious.** Not sold to. Curious enough to take the next step on their own terms, without being pushed.

**Competent.** If the user has experienced the demo, they should have completed something real — even if the scenario is fictional. The first sensation of competence inside Gunimi should come from work, not from a tutorial badge.

**Ready.** When registration appears, it should feel like a natural next step — not a toll booth. The user should want to register because they want to bring this experience into their actual business, not because they have been manipulated into it.

## 2. The First Day With Gunimi

### The narrative

The first experience tells a story before it explains a product.

The user does not begin with a feature list. They do not begin with a pricing page. They do not begin with a video of founders explaining their vision. They begin with a recognizable moment from their own working life — and they see it resolved.

The story is not about Gunimi. The story is about a person trying to run their business.

Gunimi is simply what happens when that person has the right context at the right moment.

---

### Scene 1 — Recognition

**Purpose:** The user recognizes themselves.

**Emotion:** Identification, not curiosity.

The landing page does not open with a product screenshot. It opens with a statement about how business actually feels: scattered context, too many tools, too many decisions, not enough clarity.

One or two sentences. No more.

The user reads it and thinks: "Yes. That is exactly what it feels like."

This moment of recognition is the first act of trust. The user has not yet seen Gunimi. But they already feel that whoever made this understands their problem.

The design at this moment is quiet. Dark. Unhurried. No animations demanding attention. No headlines competing with each other. One thing to read. One thing to feel.

**What the user learns:** This product understands my problem without me having to explain it.

---

### Scene 2 — The Story

**Purpose:** The user sees the problem solved, not the product demonstrated.

**Emotion:** Recognition becoming aspiration.

A short film — no longer than ninety seconds. It does not narrate Gunimi's features. It shows a business moment.

A founder opens a workspace for a deal that matters. The workspace already knows what the situation is. It surfaces one clear recommendation. The founder sees who to contact, what was discussed last time, what is pending. She does not search. She does not navigate. The context was already there.

She follows the recommendation. The deal moves forward.

That is the entire story.

There is no narration explaining "our AI." There is no feature callout. There is no price reveal. There is no voiceover saying "Gunimi is different because..." 

The difference is visible. The user feels it rather than being told about it.

The film ends. No fanfare. No applause. A quiet cut to black.

**What the user learns:** This is not a CRM. This is not a productivity tool. This is something that works before I ask it to.

---

### Scene 3 — The Shift

**Purpose:** One moment in the film where the user feels something change.

**Emotion:** Surprise that is immediately followed by understanding.

There is a single moment in the film that is designed to create a small, quiet shift in the user's mental model.

It is the moment when the workspace surfaces a recommendation — and the recommendation is correct. Not impressive. Not magical. Simply correct.

The founder in the film did not configure anything. She did not import data. She did not ask for a summary. The workspace was simply ready.

This is the moment the user's brain recategorizes Gunimi. Not as a CRM with better features. As something else. As something closer to a person who has been paying attention.

That recategorization — that small shift — is the entire purpose of the film. Everything else is setting.

**What the user learns:** This system was already working before anyone opened it.

---

### Scene 4 — The Invitation

**Purpose:** The user is invited to experience it themselves, without pressure.

**Emotion:** Curiosity, not obligation.

After the film, there is one action available. Not "Sign up." Not "Start free trial." Not "Get started."

The action is an invitation to try the workspace they just watched.

The language is calm. It communicates that this is not a commitment. That the user can leave at any moment. That nothing requires an account yet.

There is no countdown timer. No "Limited spots available." No social proof designed to manufacture urgency. No "10,000 companies already use Gunimi."

Just an invitation, in plain language, to see what the workspace feels like when you are inside it.

**What the user learns:** I can experience this without committing to anything.

## 3. Transition

### The principle

The user must never feel that they have left the story and entered a software demo.

The moment the film ends and the demo begins is the most fragile moment in the entire experience. Every product that shows a beautiful film and then opens a cluttered onboarding screen loses the trust it just built.

The transition must feel like a scene change in the same film — not a cut to a different movie.

### How it feels

The demo workspace opens with the same visual calm as the film. The same palette. The same spacing. The same unhurried pace.

The workspace that appears is the same workspace from the film. The user recognizes it immediately — the same deal, the same company, the same moment. They have been transported inside the scene they just watched.

There is no tutorial overlay. No highlight animation pointing at a button. No "Step 1 of 5." The workspace simply exists, and it has already prepared something for the user to do.

The transition teaches nothing. It continues.

## 4. Interactive Demo

### Philosophy

The demo is not an exploration. It is a guided experience with the appearance of freedom.

A free exploration demo is a product design failure. It puts the user in an empty space and asks them to figure out what matters. Most users will not. They will click around briefly, feel nothing in particular, and leave.

A scripted tutorial is equally wrong. It asks the user to follow instructions rather than accomplish work. The user learns the mechanics of the product but feels none of the value.

Gunimi's demo is neither. It is a curated workspace that has one thing that needs doing — and everything the user needs to do it is already there.

The user does not explore Gunimi. The user works inside Gunimi, on a scenario that has been designed to demonstrate one complete, meaningful cycle: situation → recommendation → action → result.

### The demo workspace

The workspace belongs to a fictional founder. The company is real enough to feel familiar — a small team, an active deal, a relationship that has context and history.

The scenario is not a toy example. It is a moment with stakes. A deal that needs attention. A follow-up that is overdue. A context the founder would genuinely need before acting.

The workspace has already assembled that context. The user opens it and immediately sees: what is happening, why it matters, and what to do next.

### The first recommendation

The workspace surfaces one recommendation. Not five suggestions. Not a ranked list of insights. One thing.

The recommendation is grounded in evidence that is visible in the workspace. The user can trace the logic without being told to. "This deal needs attention — the last activity was two weeks ago and the expected close is in five days." That is the reason. It is a business reason, stated in business language.

The user reads it and recognizes that it is correct. They would have come to this conclusion themselves, eventually. Gunimi arrived there first.

### The first completed action

The user takes one action. It is a small action — a note, a task completion, a status update. Something that takes thirty seconds.

The workspace responds. Not with confetti or celebration. With a simple, honest update: the situation has changed, the next step is clearer.

The user has accomplished something. Not a tutorial step. Not a gamification badge. Something that would matter if this were a real deal.

### The first successful moment

The first successful moment in Gunimi is not the moment the user finds a feature they like.

It is the moment they realize they did not have to search for anything.

They came in. The context was ready. The recommendation was clear. The action was simple. The result was immediate.

They were never confused about what to do next. They were never sent somewhere else to get information. They never had to remember anything.

That sensation — of working without friction — is the first successful moment. It should happen within sixty seconds of entering the demo workspace.

## 5. Registration

### Why registration comes last

Most products ask for commitment before delivering value. They require an email address in exchange for the right to explore. They treat the user's attention as a resource to be extracted rather than trust to be earned.

This is backwards.

Registration that comes before value communicates: "We don't trust you to stay unless we have your information." The user senses this, even if they cannot articulate it.

Registration that comes after value communicates: "We gave you something real first. Now we're asking for the relationship." The user trusts this.

Gunimi never asks for registration before the user has experienced something worth registering for.

### The emotional state before registration

By the time registration appears, the user has:

— Recognized their own problem in the product's language.
— Watched a business moment resolved with unusual clarity.
— Experienced the workspace from the inside.
— Completed one meaningful action.
— Felt what it is like to work without context-switching.

They are not skeptical. They are not neutral. They are in a specific emotional state: they understand what Gunimi is, and they want it for their real business.

That is the only moment at which we ask them to register.

### The registration moment

When the demo experience reaches its natural conclusion — after the user has completed the first action — the workspace acknowledges it simply and honestly.

Not with a pop-up. Not with a forced interrupt. The workspace has reached the end of what it can show without a real account, and it says so — plainly, without pressure.

"You've seen what this looks like. Build your own workspace to start with your real business."

The registration form is minimal. A name. An email. A password. Nothing else. No company size. No role selection. No intended use case. No marketing opt-ins disguised as preferences.

The faster the user can register, the sooner they can bring this experience into their actual work. Every field beyond the minimum is friction we invented.

## 6. First Real Workspace

### The risk

The transition from demo workspace to real workspace is where most products fail silently.

The demo is curated, prepared, full of meaningful context. The real workspace is empty. The contrast is jarring. The user who registered with genuine intention opens their account, sees nothing, and quietly closes the tab.

This is not a conversion problem. It is a honesty problem. The demo showed a workspace with years of history. The real workspace has none. We owe the user an honest bridge between those two realities.

### The honest beginning

When a new user arrives in Gunimi for the first time, the workspace acknowledges where they are.

Not with an apology. Not with an overwhelming onboarding wizard. With a calm, honest statement: your workspace is new. It doesn't know anything about your business yet. That is normal. Here is the first thing worth doing.

Not ten things. One thing.

The first thing should be something a business owner would naturally want to do on their first day: add a deal they're working on, or a contact they're actively managing. The workspace does not ask them to configure settings. It does not ask them to import a CSV. It asks them to do one piece of real business work.

### Preserving momentum

The momentum from the demo is not built on novelty. It is built on the sensation of clarity. That sensation can be preserved in an empty workspace if the workspace itself is clear.

Even before the user has added any data, the workspace should feel calm and purposeful. The structure should be immediately familiar — the same workspace architecture they experienced in the demo. They know where things live. They know what the tabs mean. They know what a decision card looks like when it surfaces.

The familiarity of the structure across demo and real workspace is the bridge. They are in the same product. It is simply their product now, at the beginning of its story.

### The first real action

The first real action in Gunimi is not a setup task. It is not "complete your profile." It is not "connect your email."

It is a business action. Adding the one deal that matters most right now. Recording the first note from a meeting they just had. Linking a contact to a company they're working with.

The workspace should make this feel natural — not like filling in a form, but like starting a conversation with a system that will remember everything.

When the user completes the first real action, something honest happens: the workspace becomes slightly more ready for them. Not dramatically. Not with a congratulations message. The workspace simply has more to work with now. It becomes incrementally more useful.

That is the experience of building a working relationship with a system. It takes time. It starts small. It compounds.

## 7. Design Principles

### Calm Software

The first experience must never compete for attention. Every motion, every transition, every piece of copy should feel like it was placed there deliberately — because it needed to be there, not because it would impress someone.

The pace of the experience matches the pace of serious work. Nothing is rushed. Nothing blinks or pulses to draw the eye. The product has confidence, and confidence is quiet.

A first-time user should leave the experience feeling less anxious than when they arrived. If the experience has raised their heart rate, something is wrong.

### Invisible Assistant

Gunimi's intelligence should be present everywhere and visible nowhere during the first experience.

The demo workspace should feel prepared — not because a wizard set it up, but because a system was already paying attention. The user should never consciously think "the AI did this." They should only notice that everything they needed was already there.

The word "AI" should not appear in the first experience. Not once. AI is not the product. The product is clarity. Clarity was enabled by intelligence that remained invisible.

### Business Before Software

Every word in the first experience speaks about business — about deals, relationships, decisions, momentum, context.

No word speaks about software. Not "modules." Not "features." Not "integrations." Not "workflows." Not "entities." The business owner thinks about their business. The experience meets them there.

If a sentence would make more sense in a software manual than in a conversation with a founder, rewrite it.

### Trust Before Intelligence

The first experience earns trust before it demonstrates intelligence.

Trust comes from honesty — about what the product can do, what it cannot do, and what the user will experience when they register. Trust comes from restraint — from not overpromising, not exaggerating, not manufacturing urgency.

Once trust is established, the intelligence becomes believable. Without trust, even accurate intelligence is dismissed.

### Explainability

Every recommendation in the demo experience comes with a visible reason. The reason is always a business fact, stated in business language. Never a score. Never a percentage. Never a confidence rating.

"This deal needs attention — the last contact was two weeks ago." That is explainability. The user can trace the logic. The logic is honest.

The first experience teaches the user, implicitly, that Gunimi always explains itself. This expectation is the foundation of the relationship.

### Zero Pressure

There is no timer. No limited beta. No "X people signed up this week." No urgency that the user did not create themselves.

The experience respects the user's pace completely. They can stop anywhere. They can come back. They can explore slowly. The product waits for them without pressuring them.

The one exception: genuine scarcity, honestly communicated. If Open Alpha slots are genuinely limited, that is worth stating — plainly, once, without drama. If they are not genuinely limited, it is not stated at all.

## 8. What We Never Do

These are the commitments we keep in the first experience, without exception.

---

**We never gate value behind registration.**

The user experiences something real before they are asked for anything. Their attention is earned, not extracted.

---

**We never fabricate business data in the demo.**

The demo workspace uses fictional but realistic data — a real company name, a real deal scenario, real relationship history. Nothing is invented to make Gunimi look more capable than it is. The demo demonstrates what Gunimi actually does.

---

**We never exaggerate the AI.**

We do not claim that Gunimi "understands your business." We do not show AI outputs that could not be produced from the available data. We do not use the word AI to describe capabilities that are simply good product design.

Every AI output in the first experience could be shown to a skeptic and be defended with data.

---

**We never overwhelm.**

The first experience surfaces one thing at a time. One statement on the landing page. One film. One workspace. One recommendation. One action. One registration form.

The user is never asked to hold more than one decision in their head at once.

---

**We never fake productivity.**

The demo does not show the user an empty calendar magically filling with tasks. It does not show a "productivity score" improving. It does not animate numbers going up. Real productivity is quiet. Real productivity is finishing the thing that needed doing.

---

**We never manufacture urgency.**

No countdown timers. No "Join the waitlist before it closes." No social proof that functions as pressure. If the user leaves, they leave. The product will still be here when they return.

---

**We never announce the AI.**

In the first experience, Gunimi's intelligence is present without being labeled. The workspace is prepared. The recommendation is ready. The context is assembled.

The user notices that everything was ready. They do not need to know how.

---

**We never show more than one recommendation.**

In the demo and in the first real workspace, we surface one recommendation at a time. Not five. Not a ranked list. Not "here are things you might want to do."

One thing. The right thing. With the reason.

---

**We never ask for configuration before delivering value.**

The first real workspace does not begin with a setup wizard. It does not ask the user to select their industry, their team size, their workflow preferences, their notification settings, or their integrations.

It asks them to do one piece of work. Everything else can wait.

---

**We never make the user feel like they are using software.**

The entire first experience is designed so that the user feels like they are working on their business — not navigating a product. If at any point the user thinks "where do I click?" or "what does this do?" the experience has failed at that moment.

## 9. Success Metrics

Success is not measured by sign-ups, activation rates, or day-seven retention.

Those numbers matter eventually. But they are consequences of the right first experience, not definitions of it.

The definition of success for the first Gunimi experience is this:

---

**Did the user understand what Gunimi is — without being told?**

Not from a feature list. Not from a marketing headline. From the experience itself. If a user can describe Gunimi to a colleague accurately after two minutes, without having read the about page, the experience has succeeded.

---

**Did the user feel calmer after two minutes than before?**

The first experience should reduce the user's sense of overwhelm, not increase it. If the product adds cognitive load in its first two minutes, it has failed to demonstrate its central value.

---

**Did the user complete something real?**

Not a tutorial step. Not a progress bar. A piece of work. Even fictional work counts — if the user completed an action inside the demo workspace and saw an honest result, they have experienced what Gunimi is for.

---

**Did the user understand why Gunimi is different?**

Not because we told them. Because they felt it. The difference between Gunimi and existing tools should be palpable within sixty seconds of opening the demo workspace. If the user cannot articulate the difference, the demo is not specific enough.

---

**Did the user register because they wanted their own workspace — not because they were pushed?**

The emotional quality of registration matters. A user who registers because they felt genuine pull toward the product is worth ten users who registered because a UI pattern pressured them into it. The former returns. The latter churns.

---

**Did the first real action feel like a beginning, not a setup task?**

The transition from demo to real workspace succeeds if the first real action the user takes feels like the beginning of something, not the completion of an onboarding checklist. They should finish their first session and think: "I've started."

## 10. Final Vision

### What the ideal first session looks like

A person discovers Gunimi at the end of a long day. They are not looking for software. They are not in buying mode. They found a link, or someone mentioned it, or a search led them somewhere unexpected.

They read one sentence on the landing page and recognize their own situation.

They watch a short film. At one moment in the film, something shifts — they see a business problem handled with a clarity they have not seen before. They do not analyze why. They simply feel it.

They try the workspace. Within sixty seconds, they have done something. They followed a recommendation. The context was already there. They did not search for anything. They did not navigate to three different screens. The work was done.

They register. Not because a form appeared. Because they want this for their own business, and the path to getting there is clear.

They open their own workspace. It is empty. But it is familiar — same structure, same calm, same patience. They add one deal. The workspace receives it and becomes slightly more ready for them.

They close the laptop.

---

### What they think when they close the laptop

They do not think about the features they saw. Features are forgettable.

They do not think about the AI. They never knew it was there.

They do not think about the company behind the product, or the technology, or the pricing, or the roadmap.

They think about their deal — the real one, in their real business — and they think: "I need to follow up on that tomorrow." And they feel, for the first time in a long time, that the system they are using will help them do it.

---

That is the experience we are building.

Not impressive. Not overwhelming. Not clever.

Simply: ready for the work that matters.
